This page explores the meaning and importance of voters' education, highlighting how citizens can shape their governance through informed decisions, active participation, and a deep understanding of democratic processes.

WHAT IS VOTERS' EDUCATION?

Voters’ education is the process of equipping citizens with the knowledge and understanding they need to participate responsibly in elections. It teaches people how the voting system works, why their vote matters, and how to evaluate candidates and issues critically. It also helps voters recognize misinformation and make decisions based on facts rather than pressure or false claims. Voters’ education encourages informed, independent thinking so people can choose leaders who truly represent their needs. Ultimately, it strengthens democracy by empowering every citizen to vote wisely and confidently.

WHY YOUTH SHOULD VOTE?

Young people should vote because they are directly affected by government decisions on education, jobs, climate, safety, and technology. Voting gives them a voice in shaping policies that will impact their future for decades. When youth participate, they help ensure that leaders are accountable, inclusive, and responsive to the needs of younger generations. Voting also builds civic responsibility, promotes democratic values, and empowers young people to influence change rather than just react to it.

    3. Discuss with trusted peers and mentors
    4. Vote your values and priorities

    • YOUTH IN ELECTIONS

    Research on youth voting in the Philippines shows that the Sangguniang Kabataan (SK) plays a significant role in shaping civic participation among young people. Data from the 2018 SK elections indicate that there were around 20.6 million registered SK-age voters, of which approximately 13.2 million participated, reflecting a turnout rate of about 64%. Although this data applies specifically to SK elections and not national polls, it suggests that a substantial portion of youth are politically engaged when provided with structures that directly involve them.

    Studies also show that youth who have higher exposure to SK activities tend to possess greater political knowledge, confidence, and motivation to participate in elections, compared to those who are not engaged. This implies that institutionalized youth programs, leadership opportunities, and peer-led initiatives can help increase voter turnout by making political participation more relatable and accessible. However, national election data rarely disaggregate turnout by age, making it difficult to precisely compare how many youth vote versus those who abstain.

    Overall, existing evidence suggests that strengthening SK programs and civic education can positively influence youth voter participation, but more systematic monitoring and age-specific data are needed to measure its full impact—so if young people have the chance to shape their future through voting, why not start now and make their voices count?
